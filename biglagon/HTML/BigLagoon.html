BIG LAGOON IN

EL NIDO TOWN

Big Lagoon is one of the most iconic lagoons in El Nido, on Miniloc Island. Surrounded by towering limestone cliffs, crystal-clear turquoise water, and dramatic scenery, it’s often featured in traveler’s “must-see” lists. The lagoon opens through a narrow channel; large boats can’t go in fully, so visitors usually dock and then kayak or swim inside. It’s serene, photogenic, and gives a mix of awe, peace, and adventure.

Travel Tips

Go early! Boats usually start from town around 9 AM and Big Lagoon is one of the earlier stops in Tour A. Arriving earlier helps you avoid the biggest crowds and get the best calm water conditions. Also, many operators now treat Big Lagoon as a “premium” stop so there are regulated slots; book your tour ahead.

WHAT TO EXPECT

Expect clear blue-green waters, limestone cliffs that feel dramatic and imposing, and a bit of crowd especially in mid-morning. The entrance to the lagoon may be narrow; during low tide, boats may not go all the way and you’ll need to kayak or swim in from docking points. Boat rides can be bumpy depending on sea conditions. The scenery is often stunning beyond expectations.

IMPORTANT DETAILS

Big Lagoon is part of a protected marine area, meaning there are strict environmental regulations—no littering, feeding fish, or taking corals. Cellular signal is weak or nonexistent, so prepare for a digital detox while reconnecting with nature. There are no restrooms or food stalls within the lagoon itself, so bring your essentials before boarding the boat. Don’t forget waterproof bags to protect your gadgets, extra drinking water, and a dry towel for after your swim. Lastly, remember that the beauty of Big Lagoon lies in its untouched nature—so take only pictures, leave only footprints, and make the most of your winning escape in paradise.

WHAT TO BRING

Swimwear, Towel, a change of Clothes

Sunscreen, Hat, and Sunglasses

Aqua Shoes

Waterproof camera or Phone Case

Cash for fees and optional rentals

Reusable water bottle, eco-bag

BEST TIME TO GO

Dry season: December to May is the ideal window — calm seas, clearer skies, better visibility. Avoid the monsoon / rainy season (June to October) when tours may be canceled or waters rough. Early morning tours are better for light, fewer crowds, and smoother experience.

ACTIVITIES

Kayaking is the main highlight at Big Lagoon, letting you paddle through serene emerald waters surrounded by majestic limestone cliffs. You can also swim in certain shallow parts, snorkel near the entrance, or take stunning drone and GoPro shots for that picture-perfect travel memory. Many visitors enjoy combining Big Lagoon with nearby destinations like Small Lagoon, Secret Lagoon, and Shimizu Island as part of their island-hopping adventure. For a more relaxed pace, simply float and take in the stillness of nature—a moment of calm that perfectly captures what Palawan is all about.

ACCOMODATION

Since there are no accommodations directly within Big Lagoon due to its protected status, most travelers stay in nearby El Nido Town or Lio Beach. Luxury seekers often choose Seda Lio, a beautiful beachfront resort offering comfort, eco-friendly design, and proximity to island tour departure points. For budget travelers, there are plenty of guesthouses, hostels, and boutique stays such as Spin Designer Hostel and Outpost Beach Hostel, both known for friendly vibes and accessibility. Staying near the town proper makes it easier to join early morning tours, grab a delicious breakfast, and catch sunsets by the beach after a long day of exploring.

~~~~~~~~~~~~LOCAL DISH~~~~~~~~~~~~

After exploring the lagoon, food lovers can indulge in El Nido’s local cuisine that highlights the island’s fresh seafood and Filipino home-cooked flavors. Try grilled fish, squid, prawns, and the famous kinilaw, a Filipino-style ceviche bursting with citrus and spice. You can also sample Tamilok, a woodworm delicacy found in mangroves, or enjoy Chao Long, a Vietnamese-influenced noodle soup popular in Palawan. For a sweet treat, nothing beats a cold halo-halo or fresh buko juice on a sunny afternoon. Restaurants like Art Café, Trattoria Altrove, and Tambok’s are top choices for tourists craving a mix of local and international dishes with seaside views.

FUN FACT

The Big Lagoon’s water color changes throughout the day depending on the sunlight and tides, creating a dynamic palette from turquoise to deep jade. Because of its ecological importance, only a limited number of boats are allowed each day, and most tours require guests to switch to kayaks to minimize coral damage. Early morning visits are best for photography as the water is calm and the crowds are fewer. It’s also said that the lagoon’s deep center hides an ancient underwater cave system, making it a mysterious yet enchanting part of Palawan’s natural heritage.
